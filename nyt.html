The New York Times

4 key projects I've contributed to as a product design intern.

OVERVIEW

For 10 weeks during Summer 2021 I interned as a product designer for The New York Times on the MAPS (Messaging and Personalization) Team and the UXF (UX Foundations) Team. My work centered around redesigning newsletter components, creating new onboarding flows, and surveying research to create audience behavioral archetypes. Throughout the summer I worked on two design-team projects and two individual projects, each time collaborating with product managers, engineers, and other stake-holders.

CONTRIBUTIONS

My work typically involved 1.) conducting an audit of existing research to inform my design decisions, 2.) rapid prototyping in Figma, 3.) giving and receiving feedback from other designers at weekly critiques, 4.) creating high-fidelity mockups and interactive prototypes in Figma, 5.) presenting to product managers and engineers.

As a result, one of my projects has shipped, one is in development, one is in testing, and one has been presented to design executives for further feedback.

BEHAVIORAL ARCHETYPES

How do we design for reader behavior?

Duration: Ongoing
Team: Individual project + Feedback from Jeanie Kay (Lead Product Designer)
Results: Presented findings to design executives, approved for ongoing exploration

My first project was to develop behavioral archetypes for NYTimes app users. In contrast to traditional user personas, archetypes are focused on overall behaviors and mindsets users adopt throughout their engagement. Behavioral archetypes are not mutually exclusive; users often switch between types depending on the day, time, or their mood.

The archetypes will be used to better understand our readership and inform subsequent design decisions. To create these archetypes I synthesized multiple exisiting qualitative research studies on user mental models, audience engagement, reader expectations, and reader behaviors. From here I arrived at 6 key archetypes.

In addition to determining the archetypes themselves, I also created a distinct user journey for each type, mapped their projected engagement levels over the course of a week, and provided key Times products they would be most likely to engage with. I also created an example of how to use the archetypes to guide design thinking in our most used products.

I combined each archetype's breakdown and design suggestions to create an Audience Archetype Playbook. The Playbook was then presented to multiple stakeholders: product designers, product managers, and design executives at The Times, including Chief Product Officer, Georg Petschnigg.

I was encouraged to continue my archetype work. Next steps will include substantiating my qualitative research with quantitative insights, fine tuning the archetypes, and creating an action plan for integrating these types as a design framework.

TWITTER EMBEDS FOR NEWSLETTERS

How can we reimagine existing components?

Duration: 3 weeks
Team: Individual + PM and Dev Feedback
Tools: Figma
Results: New designs are in development and will reach over 15 million newsletter subscribers when shipped

The Times has over 50 email newsletters reaching over 15 million subscribers each week. Despite the high readership, our original method of placing Tweets within newsletters was severly inefficient. Due to the technical constraints of email newsletters (HTML and CSS only) we could not use traditional Twitter embeds which would require JS. Text-only Tweets were then re-written verbatim. Threads or image-based Tweets were screenshotted and pasted into the newsletter with separate text containing the hyperlink to the actual Tweet.

Since our previous method left the content unidentifiable as Tweets, I was tasked with redesigning our method of placing Tweets within newsletters. By referencing Twitter's design system, while also sticking to The Times' native font and other design constraints, I was able to create three original Tweet embeds. The three cases I designed for were a text-only Tweet, a Tweet including an image, and a reply-thread.

To arrive at these designs I experimented with a spectrum of embed options for desktop and mobile, ranging from those most similiar to The Times' existing design system to ones more reflective of a traditional Twitter embed. I also spoke with multiple designers and engineers to more fully understand why our current designs were implemented the way they were.

After many design iterations and rounds of feedback I presented my final designs to the product manager and engineers responsible for building the embed. Once my designs were approved I placed them into developer handoff, where I outlined the spacing, size, and interaction specs. As of now, my designs are being built.

COOKING NEWSLETTER COMPONENT

Adding to our design system.

Duration: 2 weeks
Team: Joel Stillman (Lead Product Designer), Mimi Fang (Product Designer) + PM and Dev Feedback
Tools: Figma
Results: New designs are shipped and reach over 4 million Cooking newsletter subscribers.

The newest newsletter at The Times is our vegetarian cooking newsletter: The Veggie. I was tasked with creating a new component to add to the newsletter to highlight recipe variations. This portion of the newsletter is meant to be both distinct from the regular body text but also adhere to our existing font, size and color specifications.

I began my exploration by quickly designing a spectrum of component variations, ranging from those most similiar to the existing newsletter style, to those most distinct from it. I then presented my design to my team and we selected four to further explore: a colored background, checkboxes, em dashes and a vertical rule.

My team and I ultimately decided the vertical rule was the strongest design due to it's versatility. There was originally no design component for blockquotes in the Cooking newsletter design system, thus we believed the vertical rule could serve two purposes: for recipe variations within The Veggie and for blockquotes within the Cooking newsletter.

We presented these decisions to the heads of the Cooking newsletter and The Veggie, and then our design was approved for development and added to our design system.

The Veggie newsletter has since shipped. Here is the design component in action in the August 26th newsletter.

SWIPE NAVIGATION FOR ARTICLES

What are the best onboarding practices?

Duration: 5 weeks + Ongoing user testing
Team: Nate Clancy (Lead Product Designer), Lian Chang (Principal Product Designer) + PM and Dev Feedback
Tools: Figma
Results: Created a new onboarding flow for swipe navigation that is now in testing and development

The Times app is looking to adopt swipe navigation between home-page articles. This navigation would allow users to swipe left or right to change articles and swipe down to return to the home-page. At the start of my internship I helped review user testing research on how to best design this interaction. I was then tasked with creating an onboarding flow for this new feature.

I began my design process by first running a competitive audit to discover the best practices for new-feature onboarding. Typically, onboarding methods fell into one of three categories, full screen onboarding, modals and bottomsheets, and tooltips and coachmarks. Within each category methods could be arranged on a spectrum from informational to actionable depending on how much user participation they required.

From this audit I began my exploration into different onboarding approaches, separating them by user type. For a first-time user it would be the most beneficial to include swipe onboarding within our normal onboarding flow. For a casual reader who visits the app sporadically, a semi-intrusive method such as a modal or bottom sheet tutorial would work well. For an engaged reader who frequently uses the app, coachmarks would be able to onboard them the fastest.

After dozens of iterations, critiques, and conversations with other designers, my team and I were able to arrive at three distinct onboarding options to test. The goal behind this user testing would be to determine not only which option readers were most receptive of, but also to understand how contextual onboarding can begin to fit into the broaders design system of The Times. Prior to this exploration it was standard to use tooltips for any in-app messaging.

1 Modal: Our first option is a modal with instructional animations and a callout to the indicator dots at the top of the screen. This is our most disruptive option.

2 Coachmarks: Our second option is similar to the first, but this time includes coachmarks. Users could tap or mimic the swipes to move to the next screen.

3 Tooltip: Our third option serves as a control, mimicking our existing in-app messaging by using only tooltips and being the least disruptive to the reading experience.


Currently, these design options are being integrated into our user testing for swipe navigation. This will be an ongoing project into building out our onboarding practices.

REFLECTION

My summer at The New York Times introduced me to the real world technical and business constraints designers operate within. Throughout my 10 weeks I had the opportunity to conduct user testing and research, propose design strategy, create high-fidelity wireframes, Figma prototypes, give and receive design critiques, and pitch my designs.

The constant collaboration with other designers as well as product managers and engineers exposed me to the intricacies of the design process from ideation to shipping. Participating in user research reviews showed me first-hand the significance of user-centered empathetic design and solidified my interest in design as a method to improve user experience and accessibility.
