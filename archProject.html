An Architectural Intervention

Designing a public pavilion.

OVERVIEW

A course project exploring the architectural process as a progression from abstract pattern-making to the creation of an occupiable intervention. My final structure is with the intention to redesign how "walkthrough" space on campus is occupied.

CONTRIBUTIONS
I was the principal designer of the internvention. This was an independent capestone project for an architecture studio course.

TOOLS & TIMELINE
Rhino 3D
Adobe Photoshop
Adobe Illustrator
Feb - May 2020

THE DESIGN PROCESS

From paper to pavilion.

The design process navigates a variety of design techniques and building materials. It begins with using paper models to explore assembly techniques, then actualizes part-to-whole relationships via plexiglass models. The previous plexi system is then manipulated and rebuilt within a 3d powder print. Finally, the previously created system will be adapted to the site of the intervention and used to the create a physical structure.

PAPER

Techniques of assembly, surface modulation.

I began with exploring the capacities of a given material, in this case 5x5in. cardstock, to produce diverse spatial outputs. The two-dimensional surface was transformed through a series of geometric operations to gain an understanding of the relationship between geometry, material assembly, surface modulation and space.

PLEXI

Part-to-whole relationships.

Pattern and transparency defined the next round of spatial experimentation. My paper operations served as the template for a pattern of interrelated parts. After defining the "part" I developed a system so that the assembly of parts cohere into a unifying set of relationships, defining a whole, which was then actualized with laser-cut plexiglass.

ACRYLIC

Difference within sameness, figure and ground.

While the plexi system focused on repetition of a single module, this system modifies to create anomalies within the pattern. Variation was created through uniform and non-uniform scalar distortions. The first two systems dealt with material behavior (paper) and tectonics (plexi), while this system creates an amaterial form (3D powder print) that exhibits no trace of material assembly or production in order to amplify the formal qualities of the pattern.

The figure-ground serves as an exercise in understanding the relationship between solid and void. At the scale of the diagrammatic object, it makes possible the redefining of the part within the larger whole. At at an architectural and urban scale, it helps us to understand the difference between public and private, open and closed, interior and exterior, and circulation patterns within and around the space.

SITE MAPPING

Objective and analytical, subjective and visceral site analysis.

The intervention site was located in the heart of Harvard’s Campus — an elevated quad in Harvard Yard surrounded by Lamont Library, Houghton Library, and the Loeb House.

Through an objective site analysis I interviewed students, residents and tourists to understand how various constituents occupy the space. I then mapped this within a density diagram.

Through a visceral analysis I recalled my own experiences and emotions with the site in order to draw out the overall "feel" of the space.

Combining these two analyses revealed an unoccupied subspace of the site with programmatic potential for all three constituent groups. Once a field of grass, this subspace had the potential to become a major area for pause and socializing, and contrast the bustling nature of the areas surrounding it.

INTERVENTION

Address the limitations of the abstract geometric organization.

My intervention began with analyzing potential site use volumes and adapting my previous system to map to this usage. Five different sized parts correspond to the five different density zones in order to create the whole.

Begin to imbue it with architectural value.

The pattern then becomes a three dimensional pavilion roof. The roof serves as the figure, while the once negative space within it becomes actualized as a series of benches and platforms dependent on the zone they occupy.

With this intervention, this once empty space will be revitalized as a common area for outdoor socializing and pause, drawing occupants away from the busy walkways and into the canopy. Elevations, groundscapes and further renderings are displayed below.
